/ Lock In — technical documentation

PREDICT THE SCORE.
PROVE THE SETTLEMENT.

Lock In is a proximity prediction market for football scorelines on Solana. Users stake USDC on the exact full-time score; payouts are weighted by how close each guess landed. The final score enters the program only through a Merkle proof verified on-chain via CPI into TxLine's oracle — no admin, no multisig, no human referee.

/ 01

OVERVIEW

Classic sports prediction markets are binary: pick a winner, get paid or don't. Lock In asks a harder question — what will the exact final score be? — and pays out on proximity. Guess 2–1 when the match ends 2–1 and you take the biggest share. Guess 2–0 and you still beat the person who said 0–3. Skill is rewarded on a gradient, not a coin flip.

The mechanism is a Trepa-style proximity market: every staker's guess is scored by a distance metric D against the validated final score, the field's median D becomes the win gate, and losers' stakes (minus a platform take) are redistributed to winners weighted by accuracy, with per-winner caps and a water-fill pass so no single position drains the pool.

What makes it a genuine on-chain product rather than a demo:

  • Trustless settlement. The final score is accepted only with a Merkle proof, verified by a cross-program invocation into TxLine's on-chain txoracle program against the daily root TxLine itself publishes on Solana. A tampered score is rejected by the chain, not by policy.
  • One engine, three runtimes. The same fixed-point settlement engine runs in Rust (the program), TypeScript (the keeper), and the browser (live PnL) — byte-identical results, integer-exact.
  • Fully autonomous. A keeper agent discovers fixtures from the TxLine feed, lists markets, locks them at kick-off, fetches proofs at the final whistle, and settles — unattended, every 10 minutes, via GitHub Actions.
  • Conservation enforced on-chain. The program refuses any settlement where Σ payouts + platform take ≠ total pool, to the integer.

/ 03

THE SCORING MODEL

Every guess (gh, ga) is scored against the validated actual score (ah, aa) with a distance metric D. Lower is better; D = 0 means you nailed the exact score. The metric blends four football-native components:

  • Outcome — did you call the right result (home / draw / away)? Getting this wrong adds a large fixed penalty P.
  • Goal difference — how far off was your margin, capped at CAP_GD.
  • Total goals — how far off was your total, capped at CAP_TG.
  • Clean sheets — did you correctly call each side keeping (or not keeping) a clean sheet.
// guess (gh, ga) vs actual (ah, aa)
gd_guess = gh - ga            gd_actual = ah - aa
tg_guess = gh + ga            tg_actual = ah + aa
correct  = 1 if sign(gd_guess) == sign(gd_actual) else 0   // H/D/A match
d_gd     = min(|gd_guess - gd_actual|, CAP_GD)
d_tg     = min(|tg_guess - tg_actual|, CAP_TG)
d_cs     = |cs_home_guess - cs_home_actual| + |cs_away_guess - cs_away_actual|

D = (1 - correct) × P  +  W_GD × d_gd  +  W_TG × d_tg  +  W_CS × d_cs

S = 1 / (1 + D)               // similarity — display & ranking only

Default parameters — set per fixture at creation, so they can be tuned per competition:

ParamDefaultMeaning
P4.0Penalty for calling the wrong outcome (home/draw/away)
W_GD1.0Weight on goal-difference error
W_TG0.5Weight on total-goals error
W_CS0.25Weight per missed clean-sheet call
CAP_GD3Goal-difference error cap
CAP_TG4Total-goals error cap

Example: actual score 2–1. A 2–1 guess scores D = 0. A 3–1 guess (right outcome, margin off by 1, total off by 1) scores D = 1.5. A 1–1 guess (wrong outcome) scores D = 5.75. Being close pays; being right about the shape of the match pays most.

/ 04

THE PAYOUT MODEL

D plays the role of the error term in Trepa's proximity-market design. Settlement runs the following algorithm — every step of which is re-derived and enforced on-chain, not just computed by the keeper:

  1. Void checks. One staker or fewer → void, everyone refunded. Every staker at the identical D → void, everyone refunded. A void market can never lose anyone money.
  2. Best-coalition check. If the stakers tied at the lowest D make up at least half the field, they win as a group (the median gate below would otherwise eliminate everyone).
  3. Median gate. Otherwise, compute the field's median m (the k-th smallest D, k = ⌊(N+1)/2⌋). Winners are strictly better than the median: D<m. Everyone at or above the median loses their stake.
  4. Accuracy weight. Each winner gets r = D/m, then weight a = (1/(1+r))⁶. The sixth power sharply rewards precision — a near-exact guess earns a much larger share than one that barely beat the median.
  5. The pool. Losers' stakes fund the winners, minus the platform take (default 20%).
  6. Cap + water-fill. Each winner's gain is capped at 100× their stake. The pool is distributed proportional to a; any share exceeding a cap is clipped and the excess cascades to the remaining uncapped winners, repeating until stable. If every winner caps out, the residual routes to the platform take and is logged separately — nothing silently disappears.
  7. Payout. Winners claim stake + gain; losers get 0; voids refund everyone in full.
Conservation invariant

Σ payouts + platform_cut == total_pool — exactly, in integer USDC base units. The program checks this before marking a fixture Settled; integer-division dust routes to the platform cut so the books always balance to zero.

/ 05

FIXED-POINT MATH — NO FLOATS, ANYWHERE

Floating point on a blockchain is a determinism hazard and a compute hog. Every ratio in Lock In — D, r, the accuracy weight a, the formula weights — is a scaled integer with SCALE = 1,000,000 (six decimals, deliberately matching USDC's own decimal count). Intermediates use u128 to make overflow impossible at realistic stake sizes.

The accuracy weight needs an exponentiation, but with a fixed integer exponent it reduces to six exact fixed-point multiplications — no pow(), no approximation:

// r is fixed-point (× SCALE). Returns a, fixed-point (× SCALE).
fn accuracy_weight(r: u128, scale: u128, gamma: u32) -> u128 {
    // base = 1/(1+r)  →  scale²/(scale + r)
    let base = scale * scale / (scale + r);
    let mut result = scale;                  // 1.0
    for _ in 0..gamma { result = result * base / scale; }
    result
}

Every division rounds down on payouts — the program can never pay out fractionally more than the escrow holds — and the dust the rounding produces is exactly what the conservation check routes into the platform cut.

This engine lives in app/engine/ as a pure-Rust crate with zero Solana dependencies, which is what lets the identical logic compile into the on-chain program, get mirrored line-for-line in TypeScript (keeper/src/engine.ts, using BigInt), and drive the browser's live-PnL simulation. Seven unit tests pin the spec scenario to the exact integer in all three runtimes.

Two settlement paths exist because Solana transactions have compute-unit and account-count ceilings — with real staker counts, settlement cannot be one instruction. The fast path lets the keeper submit precomputed results which the program re-derives and verifies in full (median order-statistics, every distance, conservation) before accepting. The batched pipeline processes stakers in resumable chunks with cursor state stored on the Fixture, so even a market with hundreds of positions settles trustlessly.

Guards worth knowing

  • Staking requires status == Open and now < lock_time — no late bets, enforced on-chain, not in the UI.
  • validate_score requires the proof's stat period == 100 — TxLine's game_finalised marker — so a live in-play snapshot can never masquerade as the final score.
  • Stat keys must be exactly 1 and 2 — whole-match total goals home/away (includes extra time, excludes penalty shootouts, which live in a separate key series). "Final score" is unambiguous.
  • submit_settlement recomputes every staker's D from the on-chain validated score and enforces the median and the conservation invariant before flipping to Settled.
/ 08

TRUSTLESS SETTLEMENT

This is the heart of the build. Lock In never trusts anyone — not the keeper, not our own infrastructure — to say what the final score was. The score enters the program only through a Merkle proof verified on-chain against data TxLine itself publishes on Solana.

Anatomy of a proof

The object the keeper fetches from /scores/stat-validation-v3?fixtureId=…&seq=…&statKeys=1,2 and forwards verbatim:

FieldWhat it proves
leaves[]The stats being proven — {key:1, value:home, period:100} and {key:2, value:away, period:100}
leafIndices, multiproofHashesMultiproof placing the stat leaves inside the record's stat tree
eventStatRootRoot of that stat tree
fixtureSummaryThe fixture's identity + update counts — binds the proof to this fixture id

A raw sample proof is committed at proof-sample.json if you want to inspect one.

Why the keeper can't cheat

  • Wrong score → the leaf hashes change → the recomputed root no longer matches the on-chain root → the CPI returns false → ScoreProofRejected, transaction reverts. Proven live: submitting a tampered 3–1 in place of the true 2–1 fails on-chain.
  • Wrong root account → it can't pick one. The daily_scores_merkle_roots address is an Anchor seeds= constraint derived from the proof's own timestamp under the txoracle program id — the address is forced, never supplied.
  • Wrong oracle program → the txoracle account is pinned with address = to a hardcoded per-network constant.
  • In-play snapshot passed off as final → the period == 100 finalisation guard rejects it.
  • Correct score, wrong payouts → submit_settlement recomputes every distance on-chain and enforces median + conservation; a bad submission reverts.
Worst case

A malicious or broken keeper can only produce a market that doesn't settle — which resolves to Void, refunding everyone in full. It can never produce a wrong payout.

One practical note that made this viable: TxLine's v3 proofs use a shared multiproof, keeping the full validate transaction at 632 bytes on devnet — comfortably inside Solana's 1,232-byte limit, so the entire verification happens in a single CPI with no off-chain fallback needed.

/ 09

THE KEEPER AGENT

The whole market lifecycle is automated by two TypeScript agents that run every 10 minutes via GitHub Actions — no server, no ops:

  • lister.ts — pulls /fixtures/snapshot from the TxLine feed, applies listing gates (competition allow-list, kick-off window, score-coverage check, daily-root availability), and initialises a Fixture on-chain for each qualifying match.
  • monitor.ts — sweeps all listed fixtures each pass: locks markets at kick-off, watches /scores/snapshot for game_finalised, then fetches the stat-validation-v3 proof, submits it on-chain, and settles (fast path, or the batched pipeline when a void/large market requires it). Each pass writes the market registry to fixtures.json and commits it — which triggers a UI redeploy, so the site is always current.

Auth uses TxLine's free tier: subscribe → sign an activation message with the keeper wallet → JWT, cached between runs. The keeper wallet is a GitHub Actions secret; its only powers are listing fixtures and submitting proofs — and as shown above, a bad submission can't move money incorrectly.

This ran unattended through real fixtures: it listed England–Argentina from the feed, locked it at kick-off, validated the real 1–2 final via CPI, and correctly resolved it — with the bot committing the registry update on its own.

FAQ & LIMITATIONS

Is this real money?

No — this deployment is on Solana devnet with a demo USDC mint. The app mints the test USDC for you inside the stake transaction, so anyone can try the full flow with nothing but a devnet wallet and a little faucet SOL for fees.

Why do I only see a few markets?

The lister runs against TxLine's devnet feed, which carries sample fixtures rather than the full live schedule — live World Cup data is on mainnet. The pipeline is network-agnostic: mainnet is a feature flag on the program build (--features mainnet) and an env var on the keeper (TXLINE_MAINNET=1), with the oracle program id and real USDC mint swapping in. Same engine, same proofs, real fixtures.

What happens if the oracle goes quiet?

A market that can't obtain a valid finalisation proof simply doesn't settle — it resolves to Void and every staker reclaims their full stake from escrow. Funds can never be stranded by a missing proof, and no human can step in and "decide" the score instead.

Can the market maker / platform influence outcomes?

No. There is no privileged settle authority: the score must carry a valid Merkle proof (checked by TxLine's own program via CPI), the payout math is re-derived on-chain, and conservation is enforced to the integer. The platform's take is a fixed, visible parameter of the payout formula — not a discretionary lever.

What's deliberately out of scope for this submission?

This build is Market A: full-time score proximity, single chain. The broader design (player-prop markets, multichain escrows via the chain-agnostic engine, order-book style pricing, an on-chain season leaderboard) is specced in the repo but intentionally deferred — the submission focuses on proving the hard part end-to-end: trustless, autonomous, integer-exact settlement of a real feed.
